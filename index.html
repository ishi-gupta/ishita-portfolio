01 — Research

Studying how
people learn
with AI.

I believe that education — and the decisions we make around it — is what shapes the future, for better and for worse. Technology is the best tool we have to bring high quality education to everyone. Which is why I am a computer scientist who researches education. My work has been published at the SIGCSE Technical Symposium, my research is funded by a Google grant, and I've written about it in the Financial Times and spoken about it with the World Bank.

Curis Fellowship · Stanford · Junior Year
In my junior year, I was fortunate enough to be awarded the Curis Fellowship to research whatever I wanted. I wrote about how Park et al.'s generative agents paper could be used to accelerate social science research — simulating low-income classrooms in India and testing educational interventions computationally before deploying them in the real world.

That was the seed. From there I explored automated grading, then AI scaffolding for open-ended coding projects, which led to Coding Pathfinder, then the honors thesis, and ultimately the Stanford Create+AI Challenge grant — funded by Google and the Stanford Graduate School of Education.

02 — Industry

Where research
meets practice.

Nine months at Snowflake building AI agent infrastructure — while co-teaching a joint Stanford–Snowflake course on trustworthy AI agents.

For my honors thesis, I built a tool to help students learn to code. To make it reliable — to ensure it stayed aligned with the curriculum and never just handed out answers — I had to make it observable. I built what I called a multimodal adaptive prompting system: a layered architecture where different prompts activated depending on where a student was stuck, what they'd already tried, and what the course expected of them.

I didn't know it at the time, but what I'd built was an early AI agent.

Snowflake was looking for a researcher for their AI agents and observability team. They read the thesis. They liked that it wasn't just a research idea — it was fully implemented, deployed, and tested on real students at scale. They interviewed me, and I became their youngest AI researcher.

I spent the summer doing research. In the fall, I co-taught a course with them and my advisor. At the end, they offered to fund the rest of my Master's and offered me a full-time job.

I turned it down. I wanted to work on education instead.

03 — Events

Everyone
is a
developer.

From Parisian grandmas, Divinity PhDs from Harvard, and rockstars from Lyon — how a one night stand in Shanghai led me to creating global events and teaching the most unexpected people to build with AI.

The summer before Snowflake, I went to Shanghai and fell in love with an artist. We spent three months talking about capitalism, craft, and why people choose to make things. He was frightened of AI — and he couldn't see that code, which I think of as my medium, could be a craft too.

He expressed himself through dance and music. Words didn't come easily — and grants, the bureaucratic lifeline of every working artist, felt impossible to him. He hated writing. He avoided it. So when I sat with him and showed him that AI could do the hard part — structure his thoughts, find his voice on paper, turn what he felt into something fundable — something shifted. I watched him write his first grant application. He kept stopping to read sentences back to himself, surprised they sounded like him. That moment — watching someone discover a tool that removes the thing standing between them and what they're trying to do — is what I've been chasing ever since.

That conversation was the first of many. My parents. Friends' parents. A woman I met in France who ran a functional medicine practice. Each of them trying to understand tools that are reshaping the world around them. None of them developers. All of them needing a way in.

Ars Machina started as a project to bring art and machines together. Now the idea is bigger: put filmmakers, educators, engineers, grandmothers in the same room, and redefine who has the power to build. The goal is community, yes — but more than that, it's expanding who gets to be a developer. Showing a new population of builders that these tools aren't frightening. They're a wave. There's enough of it for everyone to surf.

06 — World

Languages.

When I was eleven, my family moved to China. To actually live there, you had to speak the language — so I spent two years learning. Spanish came later, required by school. But after three languages, something clicked. I got hooked on travel, on foreign films, on the feeling of a world opening up.

Working and making events in France meant learning French too. Each one harder than the last, and more worth it.

Language learning is difficult. But it keeps you present — focused on body language, on tone, on the person in front of you. It keeps your neuronal pathways alive. That's reason enough.

07 — Body

Learning is not
only in
the mind.

Humans have neurons all over them. It would be a shame not to use them all.

I love movement — especially the kind that gets me outside and into nature. Trail running, surfing, rock climbing, freediving, triathlon. Dancing, yoga, meditation. I completed a Vipassana retreat.

I think these practices aren't separate from the work. They're what make the work possible. The only way I've been able to stay curious, do many things, and push hard is by also sleeping well, eating well, and moving my body every day. To live a good and full life, you have to tend to all of it — not just the mind.
